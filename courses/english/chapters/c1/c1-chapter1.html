Chapter 1: Academic English

Academic Writing Structure

Academic writing follows a specific structure that helps present ideas clearly and logically:

Section Purpose Key Elements
Introduction Present the topic and thesis statement Background information, thesis statement, outline of main points
Body Paragraphs Present and analyze evidence Topic sentences, evidence, analysis, transitions
Conclusion Summarize and restate thesis Summary of main points, restatement of thesis, final thoughts
References Cite sources List of all sources cited in the paper

Advanced Connectors and Transitions

At C1 level, you should use sophisticated connectors to enhance the flow of your writing:

  • Adding information: Furthermore, moreover, in addition to, as well as, not only...but also
  • Contrast: However, nevertheless, nonetheless, on the contrary, in contrast, whereas
  • Cause and effect: Consequently, as a result, therefore, thus, hence, due to, owing to
  • Examples: For instance, to illustrate, such as, particularly, specifically
  • Time and sequence: Subsequently, meanwhile, initially, ultimately, eventually
  • Emphasis: Indeed, certainly, undoubtedly, clearly, notably
  • Concluding: In conclusion, to summarize, overall, on the whole, in summary

Critical Thinking in Academic English

Critical thinking is essential for academic writing and involves analyzing information objectively:

  • Evaluating sources for credibility and relevance
  • Identifying assumptions and biases
  • Distinguishing between fact and opinion
  • Recognizing logical fallacies
  • Developing and supporting well-reasoned arguments
  • Considering alternative perspectives

When writing academically, use phrases like:

  • "This suggests that..." rather than "I think that..."
  • "Evidence indicates..." rather than "It seems to me..."
  • "Research demonstrates..." rather than "Everyone knows..."
  • "It could be argued that..." when presenting alternative views
