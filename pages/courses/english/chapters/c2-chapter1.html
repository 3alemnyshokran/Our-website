C2 Chapter 1: Mastering Nuance and Advanced Rhetoric

At the C2 level, you can understand with ease virtually everything heard or read. You can summarize information from different spoken and written sources, reconstructing arguments and accounts in a coherent presentation. You can express yourself spontaneously, very fluently and precisely, differentiating finer shades of meaning even in more complex situations.

Rhetorical Devices and Stylistic Features

At C2 level, you should be able to recognize and use advanced rhetorical devices:

  • Parallelism: "Ask not what your country can do for you; ask what you can do for your country." (John F. Kennedy)
  • Anaphora: The repetition of a word or phrase at the beginning of successive clauses. "We shall fight on the beaches, we shall fight on the landing grounds, we shall fight in the fields..." (Winston Churchill)
  • Hyperbole: Exaggeration for emphasis. "I've told you a million times."
  • Litotes: Understatement, typically employing a double negative. "That's not insignificant."

Nuanced Expression

C2 speakers can express subtle distinctions in meaning, sentiment, and tone:

  • Using precise adjectives to convey subtle differences: "irritated" vs. "exasperated" vs. "incensed"
  • Employing modal expressions to convey precise degrees of certainty: "He must have been delayed" vs. "He might well have been delayed"
  • Using sophisticated hedging language: "It would appear that," "One might reasonably conclude that"

Practice Questions

1. Identify the rhetorical device in this sentence: "It was the best of times, it was the worst of times, it was the age of wisdom, it was the age of foolishness."

Answer: Parallelism and antithesis

2. Replace the underlined words with more nuanced expressions: "The criticism made the author very angry and the review was very bad."

Answer: The criticism left the author incensed/outraged/indignant and the review was scathing/excoriating/devastating.

3. Rewrite this sentence using sophisticated hedging language: "I think the government's policy will fail."

Answer: It would appear that the government's policy might well be destined to fall short of its intended objectives.
